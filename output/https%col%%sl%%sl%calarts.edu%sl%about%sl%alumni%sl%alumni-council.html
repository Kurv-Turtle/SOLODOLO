Seed Grant Application

 

Alumnx Council Seed Grants

Alumnx Council Seed Grants support ongoing projects, events, and happenings by CalArts alumnx. These grants foster community, cultivate inclusion and broaden our community’s artistic impact globally.

We encourage proposals that are experimental, reflect our community’s diversity, and are most likely to come to fruition. This initiative values partnership among CalArts, the alumnx community, and the Alumnx Council.

Grants may be applied toward expenses such as production, equipment, rentals, supplies, hospitality, printing, promotions, etc. Proposed projects should be open to and shareable with the alumnx community, including weblinks, prints, exhibits, RSVP and/or registration (for events).

Grants of $500 will be awarded in the Spring of 2022. These grants are funded through donations to the Alumnx Council Seed Grants program.

Graphic Design Alumnx Grants

The program in Graphic Design is offering ten $500 grants for graphic design alumnx in 2020–21 (two grant cycles with five awards each). These grants support alumnx in realizing self-directed projects. The grants are made possible by funds generated by graphic design faculty members and alumnx, including Yasmin Gibson MFA ’05, Roman Jaster BFA ’07, Anther Kiley, Randy Nakamura MFA ’05, Louise Sandhaus BFA ’93 & MFA ’94, Gail Swanlund MFA ’92,  Lorraine Wild, and Michael Worthington MFA ’95.

Applicants must be an alum of the CalArts Graphic Design program to apply.

Seed Grant applications are evaluated by the Alumnx Council.  Not every proposal is funded. Funds are disbursed approximately 2-3 weeks after award notifications are made and all necessary paperwork has been submitted from the grantee to CalArts. 

It is the responsibility of the recipient to responsibly steward awarded funds and consult their tax professional regarding declaring such funds.

If you are awarded a Seed Grant, you will not be eligible to apply again until a year from your awarded cycle.  For example, if you are awarded a grant in Fall 2021, you cannot apply for the Spring 2021 cycle, but will be eligible again to apply in Fall 2022

Tips for applying 

Seed Grant Projects Applications are reviewed and recipients are selected based on how they meet the goals and purpose of the Seed Grant. The criteria include:

  1. The Project is artistically compelling
     
  2. ​The Project invites/engages the CalArts Community as well as broader communities/audiences
     

  3. The Project concept is logistically well-developed. (Execution plan is likely to be implemented, the applicant demonstrates a concrete plan to promote this project, and the time frame is feasible)

  4. The Project is collaborative involving multiple CalArtians. (Project is multi- or interdisciplinary, collaborators are well defined, and key players in the project are well defined)

  5. The Project embraces our diverse alumnx. (Age, gender, geographic, nonbinary, marginalized communities)

  6. The Seed Grant will be helpful to this/these alumnx artist/s
    ​

Here are some additional tips for your application:

  • Supporting materials should include screenshots
  • Complete all application questions
  • Have a well-defined marketing plan
  • Address the feasibility of the completion of your project
  • Check out Grant Funding Basics in CalArts Connect to learn more about grant writing for your art practice

Grantee Deliverables

We ask grantees for the following as part of the partnership with the alumnx council and wider alumni community: 

  • Ability to share the product/project/event with the alumnx and the CalArts community and invite attendance/participation.
     

  • Images of the product/project/event sent to alumni@calarts.edu within a week of completion

  • Documentation of project/event examples includes process documents, copy of the work, attendee sign-in sheets, event registration printouts, etc.

  • A brief write-up about your project and experience with receiving a grant following the award

*These items will be utilized by the alumnx engagement office to help demonstrate the community impact of the grants program.

2021 Spring Alumnx Council Seed Grant Awardees

  • Cori Bratby-Rudd, Walking Amongst the Rubble: Undocuqueer Pride (A Podcast!)
    LA’s freshest free monthly queer poetry reading series, Influx Collectiv, welcomes Influx Collectiv: the Podcast to all streaming platforms near you. Season one: Walking Amongst the Rubble: Undocuqueer Pride, will include poetry performances and interviews by LA-based award-winning undocuqueer poets. Fans of the live/in person series can now enjoy the poetry they love from their bedrooms or their commuter vehicles.

  • Nadia Haile, Storytwigs Interactive Adventure
    Storytwigs creates choose-your-own-adventure style interactive stories, where the reader gets to decide what happens next. The goal of this project is to design a fully-fledged prototype of one such interactive story for Storytwigs. This builds on old-school text adventures and develops them further for an increasingly screen-native world. Even within the niche interactive fiction community, very few of the most celebrated interactive stories make an effort to create well-designed content.

  • Patrick Jewell, Brooklyn Living Room Sessions
    A video and audio podcast series connecting Brooklyn’s innovative improvisational musicians to local/international communities by way of collaborative performances and informative, entertaining interviews. This series will exhibit undiscovered (and a few world-renowned) creative musicians at a time when artists are eager to get their music to enthusiasts, and a behind-the-scenes view of the living room session that happens every day across Brooklyn will provide a unique audience experience.

  • K Knittel, OPaf (Other Places art fair)
    Fluid in definition, bonded by an intention to operate in other places outside the traditional art gallery systems, OPaf (Other Places art fair) provides an alternative art fair structure designed specifically for unconventional projects. Now entering its fourth year, OPaf features participants presenting site-specific booths encapsulating their projects and programming, representing the growing movement of hard-to-define art spaces.

  • Kesiah Manival, With Love, Escargot
    A seven minute stop motion animated film, chronicling a conflict between two individuals and the way their emotions manifest around them throughout their environment. Ambient and heavy visual energies using a blend of stop motion and experimental multimedia techniques serve to create a deeply confrontational feeling on screen of what it is to fail to communicate our emotions to those around us.

  • T.J. Tario, Drop The Beat
    The project is a short dance film that innovates the art of drag, classical music, and the dance style of Vogue Femme (from LGBT+ Ballroom subculture - and in collaboration with the Iconic House of Xtravaganza). What makes this short film unique is that T.J. Tario, as a Film Composer by day and drag performer by night, has the autonomous decisions to be in back of the camera directing, and also choreographing the vogue.

2021 Spring Alumnx Graphic Design Awardees

  • Lisa Armstrong, Echoes: Sight, Sound, and Touch
    Echoes investigates the space between joy and grief, tracking growth and healing over time. This series of installations will be comprised of projected risograph animation, a series of artist flip books, and multi-channel soundscapes. Documenting changes over time in plant matter, the work asks, how do we grow and heal over time? How are memories stored in mundane objects? And what are the memory markers that help us make meaning in our lives?

  • Jessica Fleishmann, Saturday Morning: Notes from Now for Tomorrow
    Design and production of a book that presents writings and images made by a group of women who gather weekly via zoom throughout quarantine. We reflect on our experiences during this time: the mutual support, struggles, and strengths we've found. Participants are all creative professionals in their 50s/60s, so it provides a point of view that is somewhat outside the regular social media channels.

  • Pedro Lavin, I Feel Love
    I Feel Love is a short film highlighting the completion of my coming out cycle, and my celebratory arrival into full queerness. The film tells the story of an orgasm, told in three parts, and through two distinct visual lenses. In one world, we see two male lovers sharing an intimate night of passion going from arousal to climax and finally rest; and in another, more surreal world, we witness Little Death, the god of desire, mirror those three stages through dance: birth, life and death.

  • Lucas Daniel, Problem Children
    Problem Children is an arts enrichment and mentorship program for high school-aged artists in the San Francisco Bay Area. The project pairs students with a mentor who helps them develop (over 3 months) a project of their own design and shares general creative experience and knowledge. Students also attend group sessions that introduce them to relevant artistic reference points, brings them into community with like minded peers, and pushes them to become more sophisticated creatives.

  • Jon Sueda, All Possible Futures: Unrealized Archive / Olympics edition
    All Possible Futures Unrealized Archive uncovers “unrealized” graphic design projects that consumed years of work, but were never produced. These proposals—ranging in form from PDFs to models, and sketches—remain unmade but deserving of discussion. In honor of the summer Olympics, the project will showcase unrealized Olympics logos created for host cities that were not selected. In response to the pandemic, we are visualizing this edition as a storefront window display accessible from the street.

  • Molly Surazhsky in collaboration with CalArts alums Elizabeth Dally, Juliana Romero, Elizabeth Smith , Scarlett Kim, Woohee Cho, Hannah Grossman, and more. 
    PPE for Covid-19 was created by artist Surazhsky, who has been using her sewing skills to mobilize the LA art community and CalArts alumnx to create masks for essential workers in this COVID-19 crisis. She received the first U.S. Fulbright Scholar Grant to the University of the Azores, Portugal last year, to research, write and conduct interviews for the play, which is inspired by the capes her female ancestors wore for approximately 400 years. These capes look very much like the capes in The Handmaid's Tale, and during COVID-19, it has been described as the ultimate social distancing garment.
  • Rajee Samarashinghe in collaboration with CalArts alum Christina C. Nguyen. 
    The Eyes of Summer was collaboratively developed with members of Samarashinghe’s family, shortly after the Sri Lankan Civil War in 2010. A narrative was improvised around an investigation into his mother's interactions with spirits in the village where she grew up. Landing somewhere between horror fiction and “spectral” ethnography, the film describes a population reeling from devastations of the past, where distinctions between the living and the dead are thinning.
  • Jamie Naqvi in collaboration with CalArts alum Ashish Dha, and School of Film/Video faculty Marjan Hormozi.
    In the Time of is part of an online exhibition. Climate change, the U.S. elections, COVID-19; these are but some of the challenges we face at the dawn of the new decade. The works will then be presented as an online exhibition from Sept. 4-Oct. 1st, 2020.
  • Emilia Moscoso Borja in collaboration with CalArts alums Rusty Kennedy, Marcelo Bucater, and Vivian Naranjo. 
    Emilia Desiré Quartet will record an 8-track CD of original compositions, which has been developed since 2017. The music is based on Moscoso Borja’s life experience, addressing feminism, South American culture, together with the whirlwind of everyday life. The music is very creative and boundary-breaking, music that represents Borja and, in some ways, it becomes universal so that both the musicians and the audience can assimilate, feel, and appropriate it.
  • Roger Holzberg in collaboration with CalArts alums Shannon Scrofano, Martin Casella, Joe Lancisero, Skyler Spiegel, Natalie Ferguson, Lauren Gonzales, Gabriel Solomon, and Dustin Washburn.
    Integrating Creativity Into Palliative Care and End of Life: The Healthcare By Design class is a course within the Experience Design program in the CalArts School of Theater. Each year the class partners with a hospital to deliver a pilot that solutions a real world problem for patients and families. This year the partnership is with Henry Mayo Hospital. The class is partnering with the palliative care team to develop a program that integrates creativity into the final part of the human journey, end of life. 
  • Sam Creely in collaboration with CalArts alums Yuxin Zhao, Lucinda Trask, Scarlett Kim, Anthony Storniolo, Gio Alonzi, Ana-Cecilia Alvarez, Chelsea Lauren Dright, Lisa Dring, Jess Goldschmidt, Effy Morris, Annie Render, Teo Rivera-Dundas, Hannah Rubin, Char Simpson, Meg Whitford, and Sarah Yanni.

If notation is an instrument for comprehension, preservation, and iteration, DanceNotes is an experiment in notation dedicated to refractory, malleable, and plural knowledges, and to re-contextualized genealogies of movement.

    After attending John Hogan's performance, "Permanent Staff Lecture" last year, Waggoner was struck by Hogan's prolific output of acerbically witty flyers he had created over the previous 14 years at CalArts. Waggoner approached Hogan about creating a publication to document the flyers he created from 2006-2020. Hogan agreed, and they have begun the initial scanning and design process. The book will include 50+ flyers, writings from John and other notable alumnx.
