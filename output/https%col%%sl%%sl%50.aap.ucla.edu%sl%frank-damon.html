Frank Damon – Engaging and Giving with Passion

Ginger Kan June 30, 2021 spotlightComments Closed

This year marks the Academic Advancement Program’s (AAP’s) 50th anniversary and to commemorate this special year, we are interviewing alumni, students, staff, friends, and supporters to learn more about how AAP impacted their life and cherished memories from the program’s past.

Engaging and Giving with Passion | A Visit with AAP Advocate and Supporter, Frank Damon

Name: Frank Damon

Year/Major: ’65 Political Science

Role with AAP: AAP Advisory Council Member, Advocate, Supporter

Year became active with AAP: 2006

How did you first get involved with AAP?
Frank: My longtime friends Joanne Jubelier and Jim Zidell invited me to attend an AAP Advisory Council meeting with them in 2005. The rest was history.

How has your role progressed through the years?
Frank: Thinking back I’ve been involved with the program now for almost 20 years. Since then I’ve been able to engage through the AAP Advisory Council and reading student scholarship applications. I’ve loved learning about the AAP student’s lives, how vital the program and its resources have been for them, and learn about their passions and involvement. There are a lot of first-generation students who come and are looking to excel at UCLA and AAP helps them do just that. 10 years ago, my wife Linda and I established an endowed scholarship with AAP. It makes us feelgood knowing there will always be students we are helping that are going to be amazing members of society thanks to AAP.

Are there any particular stories or memories that stand out to you over your time with AAP?
Frank: The Carter-Huggins rock comes to mind. To commemorate the assassination anniversary of Alprentice “Bunchy” Carter and John Huggins, myself and a group of AAP community members, including Masai Minters (AAP staff at the time), paid our respects by presenting a rock with engraving to honor the fallen activists assassinated in 1969. The anniversary is commemorated annually by AAP which I try to attend each year. I think commemorations are important and that event was a significant part of AAP’s history. It’s important for us to pass this history down to students so future generations can keep the legacy of AAP and Campbell Hall alive.

What does it mean for you to be a part of AAP’s history, which now spans back 50 years?
Frank: I feel proud and UCLA should too. AAP is a model program in the United States for not only increasing our diversity on campus but providing resources and a community for historically underrepresented and first-generation students. This is an incredibly useful program for students as they navigate a big place like UCLA. It provides students with a safe space and community to help them through classes, the bureaucracies of UCLA, the opportunity to learn more about themselves, and helps with student retention.

What is your favorite aspect of being a part of the AAP community?
Frank: That I can be connected to something so beneficial to students on UCLA’s campus. AAP provides a whole building and community dedicated to students with different needs. I love that all their needs will be met in that building through the different staff, students, and programs they interact with. It feels good to know that through our collective help as an AAP community that these students feel cared for and can find a home at UCLA where they can make friends and ultimately graduate and feel prepared to do great things.

What would your message be to someone who is considering making a donation to AAP but still unsure about the impact it would have?
Frank: Any donation will be well used and deserved. You can’t go wrong with giving to AAP as their needs and member base continue to grow with the diverse population of students that continue to come to UCLA each year. You are helping students with needs from scholarships to fund their educational expenses to programs that enrich their lives. AAP’s students are determined and have the perseverance and thirst to learn and grow. Our support can help them do just that.
